# The Zimka
>[!info]+ Information  
> A [[Northerners]] culture

The Zimka are a highland people of the foothills, forests, plateaus, river valleys, bogs, and lakes between the [[Sentinel Range|Sentinels]] and northern [[Sembara]]. Their homeland is [[Zimkova]], especially the upper [[Volta]], the upper [[Teft]], [[Breva]], the [[Lavnoch Plateau]], and the more isolated highlands north of the [[Great Chasm]].

Zimka tradition remembers an ancient migration across the mountains and the discovery of the [[Kestavo|kestavo]] among the bogs and lakes of the upper [[Volta]]. Modern Zimka identity is not a single kingdom or one uniform way of life. It is a shared inheritance that binds scattered northern villages, Brevan forest communities, Lavnoch clans, and the more Sembaran-influenced people of [[Ardlas]] into a broad cultural family.

## Nomenclature

"Zimka" is the name of the people and culture. [[Zimkova]] is the land most closely associated with them, though its boundaries are cultural and geographic more than political. Outsiders in [[Sembara]] often speak of the Highland Kingdoms, the Zimka Realms, or simply the highlands, especially when referring to [[Breva]], [[Lavnoch Plateau|Lavnoch]], and [[Ardlas]] together.

"Zimkovan" is used for the modern language, for people from Zimkova, and for things associated with the region. "Old Zimkovan" refers to the older Northros language preserved among more isolated Zimka communities and in ritual or antiquarian contexts.

The name Zimka can be narrower or broader depending on speaker and context. In a strict sense it points to the older highland and Brevan traditions. In a broader sense it includes all Zimkovan-speaking highland peoples with descent from the ancient Zimka, even where [[Mos Numena]], Sembaran law, or Drankorian institutions have reshaped local culture.

## Language and Naming

The oldest Zimka traditions are tied to [[Languages#~Old Zimkovan~|Old Zimkovan]], a [[Languages#Northros Language Family|Northros]] language descended from the speech of the earliest northern migrants. Old Zimkovan is now mostly confined to the most traditional and isolated parts of northern [[Zimkova]], pockets of the [[Lavnoch Plateau]], old songs, ritual speech, and the lore of the kestavo.

Most Zimka communities now speak [[Languages#Zimkovan|Zimkovan]], a group of related Eastros-derived languages and dialects descended from Old Tollish and shaped by Sembaran and Drankorian influence. Zimkovan is common in [[Breva]], [[Ardlas]], across the [[Lavnoch Plateau]], and through much of Zimkova. It preserves older Northros loan words, especially in religious speech, place names, and names of ancient kestavo.

The coexistence of Old Zimkovan and Zimkovan is one of the clearest signs of Zimka history. The older language marks the highland and northern inheritance; the newer language reflects centuries of contact with river traders, mariner-descended peoples, [[Drankor]], and [[Sembara]].

## Origins, Migration, and Settlement

The Zimka descend from the [[Northerners|Northerner]] migrations, the earliest major human movements out of [[Hkar]]. These migrants crossed the [[Sentinel Range|Sentinels]] from the west long before [[The Downfall]], speaking Northros languages and following the early intercessory traditions that became the [[Northern Folk Religions]].

In Greater Sembara, the early Zimka settled among the foothills of the Sentinels and along the headwaters and upper valleys of the [[Volta]], [[Teft]], and [[Semb]]. Their southward spread was constrained by older non-human powers, especially the lizardfolk wetlands to the south and east. Over time, Zimka communities expanded down the great river valleys and encountered Eastros-speaking mariner and river-trading peoples from the [[Western Gulf]].

By the time of [[The Downfall]], at least two related Zimka strands had emerged:

- An older highland strand in the upper [[Volta]], [[Lavnoch Plateau|Lavnoch]], and the isolated northern foothills, preserving Northros speech and the older kestavo traditions.
- A lower river-valley strand that kept religious and cultural ties to the old highland Zimka but increasingly spoke an Eastros-derived language.

After [[Drankor]] expanded into the Sembaran river valleys, the southern and eastern edges of Zimka life changed sharply. Some communities entered the Drankorian world, adopted [[Mos Numena]], or became part of the cultural ground from which Sembaran and Tollish traditions grew. In the highlands, however, many Zimka communities kept the old ways, especially where the land was hard to conquer, hard to tax, and bound tightly to local kestavo shrines.

Modern Zimka settlement varies by region. Northern Zimkova is lightly peopled, with hunters, small fortified villages, and communities clustered along the [[Volta]] and its tributaries. [[Breva]] is a forested highland realm between the [[Teft]] and [[Volta]], more organized than the far north but still fiercely independent and strongly kestavan. The [[Lavnoch Plateau]] is sparsely settled by scattered clans and small fortified villages, with pastoral life in upland valleys and seasonal pastures. [[Ardlas]], along the upper [[Semb]], is more tied to Sembara and the dwarven trade, and much less purely Zimka in religion and political life.

The northeastern edge of Zimka culture is not sharply defined. The people of northern [[Vostok]], especially the Voskalders, are described as closely connected to traditionalist Zimkova, and older records sometimes blur Zimka, Zimkovan, Voskald, and Vos identities around the upper [[Volta]] and the lakes and uplands beyond it.

## Religion

The traditional Zimka religion is the worship of the [[Kestavo|kestavo]], small local gods who carry the prayers of their communities to [[Divine Presence|Ako]], the Divine Presence. A kestavo has a home: a shrine, spring, waterfall, lake, stone, tree, village, or other sacred place. Within and near that home, the kestavo can guard a community, receive prayers, and serve as the channel through which divine blessing reaches the world.

Zimka communities traditionally establish themselves around a kestavo or a place where a kestavo can be coaxed to dwell. The shrine is therefore not only a religious site, but a mark that a village has become a true community. Great kestavo can attract pilgrims from far beyond their homes. Among the best known are [[Romil]], the glacier king; [[Kamak]], the lurker of [[Lake Kamchak]]; and [[Besla]], who is associated with the spring rains from the [[Green Sea]].

The kestavo are not the only spirits in Zimka tradition. [[Zeyfa]], the cruel west wind, and [[Vaqar]], the fire-below, are feared rather than worshipped. Their children, the vazska, are spirits of hardship, cold, mischief, war, destruction, injury, sudden death, and burning homes. Zimka custom emphasizes placating, bribing, frightening off, or avoiding these spirits rather than honoring them.

Religion differs sharply by region. In northern Zimkova, the old kestavo traditions remain strongest. In [[Breva]], kestavo worship still dominates public life, even where the region has absorbed outside influence. In southern Zimkova and [[Ardlas]], [[Mos Numena]] is often the dominant religion, though old kestavan assumptions about local divine patrons and community shrines still echo through Sembaran religious practice.

### The Great Chasm

The [[Great Chasm]] stands at the center of many Zimka fears and disagreements. Many in Zimkova regard it as the home of [[Zeyfa]] and avoid settling near it. The [[Teft]] itself, especially north of the [[Braebein]], is associated in older kestavan tradition with Zeyfa's children, and its waters were not always trusted.

Not all Zimkovans read the Chasm the same way. The [[Vejo Vaikai]], mystics of Breva who live near the [[Selgrava]], see the Chasm as a place of elemental balance and great power. The kenku of [[Kemeko Monastery]] treat the Chasm as a secluded refuge and training ground. These traditions coexist uneasily with the wider Zimka habit of taboo, warning, and propitiation.

The Chasm crisis of 1730 revealed that Zimka memory preserves truths that ordinary history has nearly lost. [[Kalima]], an Old Zimkovan-speaking woman, emerged from the Chasm near [[Kemeko Monastery]] and led the [[Chasm Explorers]] to [[Melusa]], a hidden cliffside village whose people remembered themselves as descendants of refugees from an ancient catastrophe. Their traditions had withered in isolation: the kestavo survived for many Melusans only as half-remembered stories, while sacrifice to the West Wind and fear of [[Zeyfa's Labyrinth]] shaped daily life. The rescue of the willing Melusans left a small group of Old Zimkovan-speaking refugees in need of kin, interpreters, and communities that could receive them.

## Society and Political Life

Zimka society is local first. Villages, shrines, families, clans, river valleys, forests, and upland routes matter more than centralized rule. This is especially true in northern Zimkova, where settlement is scattered and where the old ways survive most strongly. Small fortified villages, hunters, herders, woodcutters, scouts, storytellers, mystics, and shrine-keepers form the visible texture of life.

[[Breva]] is the most coherent of the Zimka realms, though even there cohesion is limited by forest, river, local loyalties, and an independent streak. The Mezhai, or woodswalkers, appear in Brevan context as scouts and defenders of the people, especially during the scattered orc raids that followed the defeat of the [[Ravager of Winter]] in 1729. The [[Vejo Vaikai]] represent another Brevan institution: mystics concerned with the Chasm, elemental balance, and the old powers of the highlands.

[[Lavnoch Plateau|Lavnoch]] is less a settled kingdom than a broad upland of scattered clans, fortified hamlets, pastoral routes, and highland villages. [[Ardlas]] is different again: Zimka in origin, but heavily shaped by Sembaran contact, [[Mos Numena]], and trade along the upper [[Semb]] and with nearby dwarven realms.

In Sembaran and Drankorian records, the Zimka are often arranged into realms or highland kingdoms. This is useful for outsiders, but it can mislead. Zimka identity is older and looser than those political labels, and many Zimka communities do not understand themselves primarily through the borders drawn on Sembaran maps.

## Historical Perspective

The ancient Zimka belong to the early human history of Greater Sembara. They were present in the highlands before [[The Downfall]], before Drankorian settlement, and before the later consolidation of Sembaran culture. Their earliest remembered traditions are older than Drankor and tied to Northros speech, local shrines, and the small gods of the land.

In the centuries before and after [[The Downfall]], contact with Eastros-speaking mariners and river traders created a lowland Zimka strand. This strand kept links to the highland kestavo religion but increasingly spoke an Eastros-derived language. Out of the same wider mixing came the cultural roots of the Tollish and, indirectly, important northern influences in Sembaran culture.

The coming of [[Drankor]] changed the south and east. Drankorian roads, law, military order, and [[Mos Numena]] spread across the lower valleys and Sembaran provinces. The exact relationship between Drankor and Zimkova is not well recorded. Some sources speak as though Drankor conquered or retreated from the highlands; others emphasize fitful expansion, local accommodation, and unclear borders. What is stable is the result: the highlands were never absorbed as thoroughly as the lowlands, and the old Zimka ways remained strongest where Drankorian power was weakest.

After the fall of Drankor, the old and new Zimka strands increasingly mingled again. Modern Zimkovan became the dominant everyday speech in many highland regions, while Old Zimkovan survived in pockets, ritual, and memory. In the modern era, the Zimka remain most distinct in northern Zimkova, [[Breva]], and parts of [[Lavnoch Plateau|Lavnoch]], while [[Ardlas]] stands as a borderland between Zimka inheritance and Sembaran life.

In the northern wars of the early 1500s, Zimka and Zimka-adjacent Voskald forces appear among the allies resisting [[Vimfrost]] and the armies from the north. Later Sembaran claims of overlordship in the highlands are difficult to interpret: some may reflect temporary military alliances, personal authority, or outsider legal language more than enduring control over Zimka communities.

## Holidays, Festivals, and Rites

Zimka rites are centered on the kestavo and on the local sacred places where those spirits dwell. The maintenance, cleaning, decoration, and protection of a shrine are communal acts. Pilgrimage to famous kestavo shrines is an important expression of wider Zimka identity, especially where ordinary village life is otherwise local and inward-looking.

Funerary custom is one of the clearest preserved Zimka rites. In kestavan belief, a soul must be found and guided by a kestavo after death. The body should be prepared quickly and buried in a sacred place where the kestavo can find it. Burial prayers praise and call the kestavo rather than dwelling too heavily on the dead, because excessive mourning may draw the soul's attention back toward the living and away from its guide.

For three years after burial, permanent grave markers are avoided. During that time the community recites the names of the dead at the opening of other rites, reminding the kestavo of those under its care while avoiding signs that might attract hostile spirits. On the third anniversary, a grave marker or artistic addition to the shrine may be made: a carved stone, a scrap of colored fabric, a mural, a statue, or another permanent sign suited to the shrine and the deceased.

Recovery of bodies matters deeply. A soul whose body is lost, burned, drowned, or left unburied may be difficult for the kestavo to find. Killing people and scattering their bodies far from the protection of their shrine is therefore among the worst violations imaginable in traditional Zimka thought.

# The Northern Tribes of the Deno'qai
>[!info]+ Information  
> A [[Deno'qai]] cultural group

The northern forests, north of the [[Blackwater Fens]] and inland from the coast, were once home to many Deno'qai, before [[Cha'mutte]] established himself in Pandar. This area, a vast forest stretching from the sea to the mountains, was north of the elven territories, and the Deno'qai who settled here were fierce, independent, and less influenced by the Elven ways. However, they suffered suffered greatly during the [[Great War]] and have only [[Timeline of the Northern Deno'qai|recently begun]] to emerge from their mountain redoubts.
### Customs

The northern Deno'qai, while practicing [[Tanshi Worship]] like their southern brethren, and descended from the same original settlers, have adapted some unique cultural practices. They wear heavy furs, and lamellar armor (scale mail), and fight with axes. They often ride reindeer, or even giant mountain goats. 

Little farming would normally be possible in the high mountains, but the Ko'zula at least have learned some of the terraced farming magics of the stoneborn, and grow hearty crops in the long days of the short summers. Nonetheless, their diet and survival depends far more on hunting than the southern Deno'qai, and mammoth hunts are major events that can feed a village for a long time. For hunting, they use long barbed javelins and bows. Each village maintains a lot of managed territory for hunting, with game animals managed for productivity. 

The northern tribes trade frequently with the Stoneborn. Village customs and rules often adopt some Stoneborn practices and ethics: to survive in the harsh northern environment, you musth do what is good for the community, not what you want to do. If you won't, or can't do what is needed by the village, you leave and cease to be burden. 

Many villages are isolated and small, but maintain contacts across the tribe through yearly gatherings; each village sends representatives. There is also a tradition of marriage outside the village, though no formalized rules for which partner moves. These customs keep connections intact across otherwise isolated small villages.

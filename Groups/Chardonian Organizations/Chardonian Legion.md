The famed Chardonian Legion is the military force of the [[Chardonian Empire]], and one of the chief instruments of imperial power, allowing Chardon to project force far beyond the settled core of the Empire. The Legion is subdivided into a variety of numbered field formations, local garrisons, road guards, frontier forts, border detachments, and other forces assigned to protect imperial interests. Commanders of its major formations report through the military hierarchy to the [[Mitus Verina Auratan|Magistros]], rather than to local proconsuls, making the Legion an agent of central control as much as provincial defense.

The Legion also incorporates elite mageguard and battle-mage units. While the details of these forces are not widely publized, but Chardonian military power is closely tied to [[chalyte]], which is said to fuel the feats of the Legion's war wizards.

## DM notes

Little of what follows is canon, but the general brainstorming and vibes should be preseved. 

### Nomeclature

The Chardonian Legion specifically refers to the professional standing army of the Chardonian Empire. The Legion itself is divided into numbered subunits, typically called Guards or Regiments (haven't fully settled yet), often with geographic or other nicknames. An example might be the Seventh Regiment, "Myraeni" or the Sixth Voltara Guard. In principle one could imagine a system where Guards are more elite -- e.g., by analogy to the Mageguard -- but not really clear yet. The exact organization of the Legion hasn't been important to any campaign to date.  

Each Guard or Regiment shoudl be subdivided further. I tend to like brigade or cohort for this. 

Garrison probably has a formal meaning of a detachment seconded to a local commander, often a proconsul. So the Voltara Garrison would be the troops under the command of the proconsul, but they might consist of individuals detached from the First Regiment, "Valandros" and the Sixth Voltara Guard. 

### Command model

The working model is that proconsuls administer provinces and cities, while commanders of the Legion's major formations hold independent military authority and report up to the Magistros. This is meant to make the Legion a tool of central imperial control, not a local militia or a private army for provincial officials. Local officials can request, coordinate with, fund, supply, and politically pressure Legion forces, but they should not directly command them. The exception would be garrisons, which might be Legion troops detached to a trusted local commander. 

Again, the details are not worked out but this is the basic vibe of Voltara-area events, as well as the Dunmar-Chardon war, where the Magistros had the ability to directly appoint commanders of the divisions marching on Dunmar.

### Northern Provinces and Chalyte

The northern provinces are an illustrative case study. Canonically, they are (were) critical to the empire's power (via chalyte; at least prior to the reopening of chalyte mines in the Chataan Mountains). But they also were also poorly prepared to fight off Grumella in DR 1747, and capable of sustaining a rebellion in DR 1749-1750. This implies that there was not a huge Legion presence despite the risks. 

The obvious explanation is not that Chardon ignores the region, but that chalyte wealth gives local elites reasons to resist too much direct imperial military oversight. The chalyte oligarchs and mine interests want imperial roads, markets, legitimacy, and protection, but may prefer local guards, private security, and pliable provincial arrangements over a strong permanent Legion command. This keeps them free to be corrupt, etc. 

This can also help explain why [[Voltara]] has visible wealth, walls, restricted gates, mine guards, caravan protection, and a substantial local guard, but still lacks the kind of integrated imperial field presence that could quickly crush [[Grumella's Horde]]. The weakness exposed by [[Grumella's War]] is therefore a political and institutional weakness, not simple imperial neglect.

After Grumella's defeat, the Legion's raids from northern forts can be read as the central state reasserting control. Later conflict between [[Chardon]] and [[Greater Voltara]] should probably draw on this same fault line: imperial reform and central oversight against frontier chalyte interests that frame their resistance as local independence.

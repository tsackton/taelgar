# Undead

%% setting status/check/mike in case you have other notes or thoughts to add, or suggestions for other categories/headings to include %%

Across Taelgar, in all cultures and among diverse peoples, there are myths, warnings, and lore about undead. Technically described as a transformation of a sentient creature in scholarly lore, undeath is a curse to ward off, and, much more rarely, a goal to seek. 

As undeath has major implications for the fate of souls, most religions have something to say about it. Across the Mos Numena-influenced world, the undead are seen as wholly evil, and destroyed on sight, but most religious scholars make practical distinctions among types of undead. In folklore and legend, these distinctions are not always obvious, and some major outbreaks of undead violence loom large in the popular imagination. 

## Undeath and the Soul

From a scholarly, theological point of view, there is a major distinction between the lesser corporeal undead, the incorporeal undead, and the greater corporeal undead, having to do with the nature of souls. 

All sentient beings possess a [[Plane of Souls|soul]], made of [[soulstuff]] and gifted by the [[Divine Presence]], whether directly or indirectly; in all known religious texts, it is understood that this soul seeks to return to the divine after death. Many [[Traditions around Death|religious traditions]] aim to ease this journey, which requires the soul to enter the [[Land of the Dead]], and navigate through that place to reach the [[Divine Realms]]. 

### Lesser Corporeal Undead 

The lesser corporeal undead, such as zombies and skeletons, are theologically unproblematic, as these are simply reanimated dead bodies devoid of souls. Most cultures still consider the magic required to reanimate the dead to be unsavory, if not downright evil; in some cases, such as ghouls and ghasts, the reanimation process can instill an insatiable hunger for the living. To the extent that these reanimated bodies have intelligence and will, it is, like other magical constructs, provided by the animating magic itself, not [[Soulstuff]]. 

### Incorporeal Undead

The incorporeal undead, on the other hand, are the sentient trapped souls of creatures that died, but were unable to reach the [[Land of the Dead]]. This can be for many reasons, both intrinsic to the dead soul (as is common, for example, with ghosts) or extrinsic (as is, for example, the case for specters created by the killing blows of some powerful evil entities); these undead are often associated with violent deaths and curses that bind souls. In all religious traditions known, the persistence of a soul on the [[Material Plane]] after death is seen as a great evil, that must be prevented. 

### Greater Undead

The most terrifying undead of all are those who sought their condition. Only confused rumors and conflicting myths exist of the processes that allow a sentient being to cheat death and persist as a conscious, willful entity in a dead body, but all agree this is a wholly evil act. Powerful mages who complete a ritual to bind their souls to an object on the [[Material Plane]], such as [[Apollyon]] and [[Malach]], are called liches and are perhaps the most feared of all the greater undead. Some rumors suggest that [[Umidzka]], one of the [[Three Despairs]], aids some of those who seek this path. 

## Folklore and Mythology

While most common folk hope to never have experience with undead themselves, folklore and stories are shaped by disasters, and often focus on the mindless, relentless, unreasoning nature of the unliving. 

In Sembara, people still speak with dread of the [[Undead Attacks in Sembara|undead uprisings]] during rule of [[Robert I]]. Stories speak of the winter when graves opened, drowned things stirred, and the dead walked in scattered waves across Wisford, Dunfry, Ainwick, and many other places along the [[Enst]] and [[Wistel]]. Those who still remember those days speak of skeleton armies besieging [[Fellburn]] and Gowerborne and hamlets where the walking dead outnumbered the living. Priests still cite that season when urging proper rites for the dead. 

In Dunmar and in Nardith, it is rumors of the skeleton lord [[Grash]] and his [[Grash's Horde|undying army]] that shape people's fears. Stories come west from the deserts beyond [[Karawa]], telling how Grash’s fallen orcs rose behind him until his war-host became a horde of corpses. The tales speak of the dwarven hero [[Riswynn]], whose divine might, channeling the power of the [[Bahrazel]], held the dead at bay for weeks, the only thing keeping the living from being swallowed by a horde of hungry corpses. Far from the cities that survived him, Grash is spoken of as proof of the terrible power of the undead marshaled under the banner of war.

## DM Notes

A few other notes:

(1) The distinction between "animated body", "trapped soul", and "greater undead" is theologically important and any undead should at least conceptually be linked to this categorization. Fortunately this is usually straightforward with D&D rules. Zombies, skeletons, ghouls, ghasts, and other low-CR corporeal undead fall easily into the "lesser corporeal undead" class. Anything that is incorporeal is incorporeal (duh). Greater undead can have a much higher diversity of form and type. More details:

- Lesser corporeal undead (e.g., skeletons, zombies and the like) which are basically animated objects and in some ways not that different any other "animate something" spell. Though most cultures see these as at least morally questionable, there is certainly space for a culture where animating the dead is much more normal, and necromancers don't have to be inherently evil (one could imagine a necromancer that plays more as strange than evil). To the extent these undead have intelligence, it has nothing to do with the dead person, it is created by the magic that animates them; they don't have souls. Some of these -- most introduced so far -- are created with a mindless desire to destroy life, and that tends to define some types, like ghouls/ghasts. but this isn't required, metaphysically. These undead often serve a master, but aren't required to. 
- Incorporeal undead (e.g., specters, wraiths, ghosts, etc) are by definition the trapped souls of things that died but are prevented from leaving to the [[Land of the Dead]]. They are often created by more powerful undead when they kill something (e.g. the numerous statblocks that create a specter on death). They are also associated with curses, violent deaths, and similar things. The plague hauntings, for example, are mostly incorporeal undead. These are also good models for "haunted spirits on a battlefield" type things. 
- Greater undead (e.g. mummy lords, liches, vampires, Grash) are generally speaking the result of someone who make a conscious choice to work to become undead and so never actually "died", just passed from life to undeath. [[Grash]], [[Apollyon]], and [[Malach]] are obvious examples here. These are typically corporeal, but it isn't obvious this is required by the metaphysics (but note there aren't really traditional high-CR incorporeal undead in D&D). 
- Vampires are a bit of a special case. They are cool villains but otherwise don't quite match the vibe of the rest of the undead in Taelgar. So far, the only vampire canonically introduced in game is [[Rhodar von Glauer]], who is tied to [[Ursk]]. This could raise the possibility of a very different metaphysics, either tied to Urskan magic somehow, or perhaps best thought of as an alternate kind of lichdom. Though I don't think we should rule out "transmissible vampirism" entirely yet. 

(2) On the topic of necromancy, i think there is an important distinction between mindless corporeal undead (skeletons, zombies) which don't affect the soul at all, and things like ghosts, specters, wraiths which represent a trapped soul unable to reach the [[Land of the Dead]]. Plenty of people would see the former as a violation and evil, and I would imagine that cremation and/or burial on sacred ground would be commonly seen as a way to prevent that. However, the soul and the gods don't necessarily care - this is just human superstition around death. The latter has nothing really to do with the treatment of the body, as least not metaphysically.

- This leaves open the possibility of cultures with different attitudes towards undead. Unlike enchantment magic, which I think is kind of universally at least dubious if not outright evil (since it involves taking away free will / consent), animating corpses into zombies or skeletons could be seen as perfectly normal.Mmaybe the undeveloped islands west of Chardon have a necromantic tradition, for example. 
- However, forcibly binding the soul of a dead creature to remain on the material plane and serve you - i.e., creating specters / wraiths - is akin to the worst kinds of enchantment magic and should be universally coded as evil, I think. 

(3) The role of [[Ghesyn]] is fairly unresolved; basically just a brainstorming note proposing some kind of association between Ghesyn and the curse of undeath. One option is to view Ghesyn as mostly animating the kind of mindless lesser undead who nonetheless have a strong "kill the living" vibe (e.g. things like wights and ghasts). Another, or additional, option is to consider Ghesyn as kind of a patron of the greater undead and a component of the rituals that allow people to undergo this transformation willingly.

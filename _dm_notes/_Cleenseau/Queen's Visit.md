Celyn—

Addermarch is all mud and sheep and, somehow, fey nonsense. I went poking after a rumor and found a whole little knot of it in a small village in the Westcliff. The local priest keeps old stories and listening to her reminded me of home. You would like her, I think. She brooks no nonsense but has a soft spot for quiet souls. Her nephew reminds me of your brother. While passing through I heard a tale I thought you should know, for she mentioned the Enst and the towns along it, and I know you've been up that way.

She told of an ancient lord of the brambles and briars called, by her, the Briarheart, who she says has lived in the Feywild since the dawn of time. Her job, she says, is to appease him, to keep the fey passages closed to worse things that might come through, like the minions of the [[Lord Umbraeth|Gloomshaper]] - a dangerous fey lord up long the Enst. A few years ago, she said, a trouble came from the Fey lands, despite her efforts - she called it the Nightsong Roots. Worgs, she said, came through, nasty and with a taste for revenge on mortals. She told of a group of  strangers who arrived at the exact wrong time and then saved them anyway, and they talked about it the way villagers do—half gratitude, half awe, half fear of the next time. These strangers, she said, managed to close the Nightsong Roots with fire and magic, and the troubles vanished. She was grateful, but feared to talk too much of it, I think there was some bad blood in the village at the time. 

These stories of Briarheart intrigued me, and I followed some other tales. It seems all over Westcliff and western Addermarch are tales of the Briarheart, feared and appeased. They don't think of the fey the same way here as back at home - they are scared of them, mostly, and appease them to keep them away. The rituals are not so different, but it is all tinged with fear, not joy and gladness and respect. Briarheart seems to be a source of many ancient traps and portals, leading the way to his realm. Few seem to come back from them, although many here say he can be appeased. Some rumors say his portals used to spread far north and west of here - so keep an eye out, especially if you see a warning stone. It is said the Briarheart used to cause these gates, but no one seems to know what happens to them once his attention fades. 

Write me when you can. I miss you.

—Ellis

Captain—

The passage is not an arch or standing stone - although I did see the buried remains of an ancient stone, now much hidden under tree roots. I did not uncover it, but perhaps one could. It is a *maze*—a tight, thorn‑walled labyrinth that grows upon itself. On our side the briars form a hollow that swallows sound; travelers who step within lose direction almost at once, as if the paths shift when unobserved. There is always a denser heart to it, a bramble knot in the center. When the gate is *active*, the maze does not end in thorns, but in a breath of cold twilight. When it is *inactive*, the maze ends in itself, and you walk out where you entered, convinced you have gone somewhere else.

On the other side the character is the same: a thorn‑maze under a dark, wet sky, the air smelling of old leaf‑mold. The bramble there is thicker and more deliberate, as if grown to *contain* rather than merely to confuse. It is a place where you can feel the land resisting you. I would not call it hostile, but it is not welcoming.

We observed fluctuations in its behavior. Some days it sits quiet and inert; on others the bramble tightens, and the center “breathes” with cold. When it does so, small fey creatures keep their distance. The creatures we spoke to were evasive but consistent: *something is pulling on this place from far away.* They used no name I can trust.

Practical notes: the maze can be burned back, but regrows quickly if the gate is active; when inactive, fire takes and leaves it dormant longer. The heart of the bramble reacts to song and resonance. Discordant tones unsettle it; harmonies seem to open it. If you must work near it, do so with a musician and a flame.

I do not think the gate is stable. It feels tethered by habit rather than by will. If some hand seeks to anchor it, that act will leave a mark we can follow. Until then, it is a wound that does not close and a door that does not always open.

Vahaiya
aistana elyë imíca

Preparing concise notes
(a) Old letter, Tyrwinghan earl (second‑hand):
“—I have only the tale as it reached me: a wine‑merchant’s clerk swears his mistress visited the Twilight Court, and met a woman there who called herself Sarabeth Asa, thin as a reed but very much alive. He said she spoke of a thorn‑maze fleeing across the Sunset Gate. It is only rumor—but rumor with a name and a face.”

(b) Son’s scouting notes, Cleenseau gate:
“South wood: briar‑maze again. It pulls—not like wind, more like the path leans. At the heart there is a hush, and the air tastes of cold iron and wet leaf. There are times the thorns ring, faint, like distant chimes. If I stand still I feel it tug eastward; if I step back the tug lessens. This is the place that took her.”

(c) Court note (inheritance delay):
“Petition entered: Asa holdings in Asineau. Counter‑claim filed citing credible reports that Sarabeth Asa and her son yet live in the fey realms and have been seen beyond the Sunset Gate; estate to be held in trust until the truth is settled. Counsel notes: ‘missing is not dead in such matters.’ Petition denied”

(d) Letter to Eleanor from Peter de Brune (Sunset Gate description):
“Eleanor, I write because you knew Sarabeth. A young Asa passed through our lands by the Lord Serenveil’s grace. He spoke softly of a bright court where twilight tastes of honey, and of a crossing called the Sunset Gate—an arch of boughs that only opens when the sky burns gold and the birds fall silent. We helped him find the right hour and the quiet path; he crossed without harm. If you have faith, know this: the way out exists.”
